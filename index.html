NASA SDO / ESA SOHO / NOAA SUVI
Images from NASA SDO (Solar Dynamics Observatory) in geosynchronous orbit, and SOHO at the L1 point. SDO updates every ~10 min at much higher resolution than SOHO.

SDO 304Å: Chromosphere at ~50,000 K. Shows prominences, filaments, and flare ribbons.

SDO 193Å / 171Å: Corona at ~1–1.5 million K. Dark regions = coronal holes that blast fast solar wind.

HMI Magnetogram: Magnetic field map of the photosphere. White/black = opposite polarities. Complex regions produce flares.

LASCO C2/C3: Coronagraphs blocking the Sun to reveal CMEs. Bright halos = possible Earth-directed CME.

SUVI Map: NOAA's GOES-16 solar ultraviolet imager composite.

NOAA SWPC
This chart shows everything happening to Earth's magnetic environment. Reading top to bottom:

Btotal / Bz (top): Interplanetary magnetic field. When the red Bz line dips negative — geomagnetic storms fire up.

Density (2nd): Solar wind particle density. Spikes = a denser plasma cloud (CME sheath) hitting Earth.

Speed (3rd): Solar wind speed in km/s. 500+ with southward Bz = storm conditions.

Temperature (4th): Solar wind proton temp. Sudden rises signal CME shock fronts.

Kp (5th): Cyan = forecast model, green bars = measured Kp. 5+ = geomagnetic storm in progress.

DST (bottom): Disturbance Storm Time. Dips to −50 nT or more = genuine geomagnetic storm.

NASA DONKI
From NASA's DONKI database:

CME (Coronal Mass Ejection): A massive plasma burst from the Sun. If Earth-directed, causes geomagnetic storms in 1–3 days. Speed matters — faster = harder impact.

Solar Flare: Sudden brightness flash. Classified A, B, C (minor), M (moderate), X (major). X-class = immediate radio blackouts and radiation storms.

NOAA SWPC
Official NOAA SWPC alerts function like weather alerts for space weather:

Watches = conditions possible in coming days. Warnings = event imminent or in progress. Alerts = threshold crossed (e.g., Kp hit storm level).
