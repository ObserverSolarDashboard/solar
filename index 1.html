Planetary Kp Index
Geomagnetic activity
NOAA SWPC
The Kp Index measures geomagnetic disturbance on a 0–9 scale. It tells you how agitated Earth's magnetic field is, driven by solar wind hitting our magnetosphere.

Why it matters: Higher Kp = stronger geomagnetic storm. At Kp 5+ you may see aurora at mid-latitudes. At Kp 7+ expect GPS errors, power grid stress, and HF radio blackouts.
0–3 Quiet 4 Active 5–6 Storm 7–9 Severe
Solar Wind Speed
km/s
NOAA SWPC
Solar wind is a constant stream of charged particles from the Sun, normally ~400 km/s. CME impacts or coronal hole streams can spike it to 800+ km/s.

Why it matters: Faster wind = more energy hitting Earth's magnetosphere. Speeds over 500 km/s often trigger geomagnetic storms, especially when combined with southward Bz.
<350 Calm 350–450 Elevated 450–600 High >600 Extreme
Bz Component (IMF)
nT — Interplanetary Magnetic Field
NOAA SWPC
Bz is the north–south component of the interplanetary magnetic field carried by the solar wind. Think of it as the "key" that unlocks Earth's magnetosphere.

Why it matters: When Bz goes southward (negative), it opposes Earth's field and lets solar energy pour in — this ignites geomagnetic storms. Northward (positive) = Earth's shields hold.
>0 Northward 0 to −5 Mild −5 to −10 Storm likely <−10 Severe
Solar Radio Flux (F10.7)
sfu — Solar Flux Units
NOAA SWPC
F10.7 Solar Radio Flux measures microwave radiation from the Sun at 10.7 cm wavelength — the single best proxy for overall solar activity and sunspot count.

Why it matters: Higher flux = more active Sun with more sunspots, flares, and CME potential. Solar minimum ~70 sfu, solar max 200+.
<100 Low 100–150 Moderate 150–200 High >200 Very high

NOAA SWPC
NOAA classifies space weather into three scales:

R (Radio Blackouts): From solar flares. X-ray bursts ionize the atmosphere and disrupt HF radio. R1 = minor fade, R5 = complete HF blackout for hours.

S (Solar Radiation Storms): Energetic protons from the Sun. Dangerous for astronauts, can damage satellites. S1 = minor, S5 = extreme radiation hazard.

G (Geomagnetic Storms): Disturbances in Earth's magnetic field from CME impacts. G1 = minor aurora, G5 = widespread power failures and aurora near the equator.

ESA/NASA SOHO
Still images from SOHO (Solar and Heliospheric Observatory), a spacecraft at the L1 point between Earth and Sun. Updated roughly every 15 minutes.

EIT 304Å: Sun's chromosphere at ~50,000 K. Shows prominences, filaments, and flare ribbons. Bright spots = active regions.

EIT 195Å / 171Å: Corona at ~1–1.5 million K. Dark regions are coronal holes — openings that blast fast solar wind toward Earth.

LASCO C3 / C2: Coronagraphs blocking the Sun's disk to reveal CMEs. A bright halo expanding outward = possible Earth-directed CME. C2 = close view, C3 = wide field.

EIT 284Å: Hottest coronal plasma at ~2 million K. Highlights the most energetic active regions.

NOAA SWPC
This chart shows everything happening to Earth's magnetic environment. Reading top to bottom:

Btotal / Bz (top): Interplanetary magnetic field. When the red Bz line dips negative — geomagnetic storms fire up.

Density (2nd): Solar wind particle density. Spikes = a denser plasma cloud (CME sheath) hitting Earth.

Speed (3rd): Solar wind speed in km/s. 500+ with southward Bz = storm conditions.

Temperature (4th): Solar wind proton temp. Sudden rises signal CME shock fronts.

Kp (5th): Cyan = forecast model, green bars = measured Kp. 5+ = geomagnetic storm in progress.

DST (bottom): Disturbance Storm Time. Dips to −50 nT or more = genuine geomagnetic storm.

NASA DONKI
From NASA's DONKI database:

CME (Coronal Mass Ejection): A massive plasma burst from the Sun. If Earth-directed, causes geomagnetic storms in 1–3 days. Speed matters — faster = harder impact.

Solar Flare: Sudden brightness flash. Classified A, B, C (minor), M (moderate), X (major). X-class = immediate radio blackouts and radiation storms.
NOAA Active Alerts
NOAA SWPC
Official NOAA SWPC alerts function like weather alerts for space weather:

Watches = conditions possible in coming days. Warnings = event imminent or in progress. Alerts = threshold crossed (e.g., Kp hit storm level).
